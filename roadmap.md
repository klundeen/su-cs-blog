CS Perspectives launched in March 2026 with two pilot posts. Here's what's next.

## Beautiful Algorithms

A recurring series celebrating elegant solutions. Each post takes one algorithm, explains why it's beautiful, and connects it to something the reader cares about. Accessible to upper-division undergrads; interesting to practitioners. This is the series most open to contributions — every CS faculty member has an algorithm they love.

First entries: **Radix Sort** (breaking the O(n log n) barrier by not comparing at all), **Bellman-Ford & Arbitrage Detection** (negative-weight cycles = free money), **Bitonic Sort** (who thinks of this?), **Consistent Hashing** (how the internet stays up when servers go down), and **Embeddings** (turning words into geometry). More to come.

## AI & Teaching

Posts about what AI means for how we teach computer science. The [truck post]({{ '/2026/03/10/the-ai-knew-it-was-a-truck/' | relative_url }}) is the anchor; these extend the conversation into specific courses, specific assignments, and specific open questions. Every faculty member is dealing with this — the blog is a place to compare notes.

Planned: **Assigning Guided AI Activities** (the practical "here's how I actually do it" piece) and **Teaching Parallel Computing in the AI Age**. We'd especially welcome posts from faculty in other courses — how is this playing out in data structures? In object-oriented development? In algorithms?

## Explorations

Longer posts that chase a question wherever it goes — the kind of thinking-out-loud that doesn't fit in a lecture or a paper. The [Cantor post]({{ '/2026/03/20/from-aleph-0-to-aleph-1/' | relative_url }}) is an example. Planned: **Could My Distributed Systems Students Shut Down Bitcoin?** (a thought experiment that turns out to be a great way to teach distributed systems) and **Resource Utilization Ethics: Bitcoin, LLMs, and the Cost of Computation**.

## Site & Tools

We are also building out the site itself to make contributing as frictionless as possible.

**Done:** The [Post Composer]({{ '/compose/' | relative_url }}) lets you write a post with a live preview and export correctly formatted Jekyll Markdown — no Git required. The [How to Contribute]({{ '/contribute/' | relative_url }}) page explains three ways to submit (composer, Markdown, or just email a draft).

**Planned:** An AI writing assistant built into the composer. The idea is to let you ask an AI to help draft an opening paragraph, suggest section structure, or tighten your prose — all without leaving the tool. This requires a way to handle API credentials securely; we are exploring options including a lightweight proxy and bring-your-own-key. In the meantime, you can always draft with [Claude](https://claude.ai) or another AI tool and paste the result into the composer.

## Your post here

The blog works better as a department effort than a solo project. If you've got something you're thinking about — an algorithm, a teaching problem, a technical argument, a half-formed idea — that's enough to start. The bar is: *would this be interesting to a smart person who isn't necessarily in your subfield?*
