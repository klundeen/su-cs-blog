<p>CS Perspectives launched in March 2026 with two pilot posts. Here's what's next.</p>

<h2>Beautiful Algorithms</h2>

<p>A recurring series celebrating elegant solutions. Each post takes one algorithm, explains why it's beautiful, and connects it to something the reader cares about. Accessible to upper-division undergrads; interesting to practitioners. This is the series most open to contributions — every CS faculty member has an algorithm they love.</p>

<p>First entries: <strong>Radix Sort</strong> (breaking the O(n log n) barrier by not comparing at all), <strong>Bellman-Ford &amp; Arbitrage Detection</strong> (negative-weight cycles = free money), <strong>Bitonic Sort</strong> (who thinks of this?), <strong>Consistent Hashing</strong> (how the internet stays up when servers go down), and <strong>Embeddings</strong> (turning words into geometry). More to come.</p>

<h2>AI &amp; Teaching</h2>

<p>Posts about what AI means for how we teach computer science. The <a href="/2026/03/10/the-ai-knew-it-was-a-truck/">truck post</a> is the anchor; these extend the conversation into specific courses, specific assignments, and specific open questions. Every faculty member is dealing with this — the blog is a place to compare notes.</p>

<p>Planned: <strong>Assigning Guided AI Activities</strong> (the practical "here's how I actually do it" piece) and <strong>Teaching Parallel Computing in the AI Age</strong>. We'd especially welcome posts from faculty in other courses — how is this playing out in data structures? In object-oriented development? In algorithms?</p>

<h2>Explorations</h2>

<p>Longer posts that chase a question wherever it goes — the kind of thinking-out-loud that doesn't fit in a lecture or a paper. The <a href="/2026/03/20/from-aleph-0-to-aleph-1/">Cantor post</a> is an example. Planned: <strong>Could My Distributed Systems Students Shut Down Bitcoin?</strong> (a thought experiment that turns out to be a great way to teach distributed systems) and <strong>Resource Utilization Ethics: Bitcoin, LLMs, and the Cost of Computation</strong>.</p>

<h2>Your post here</h2>

<p>The blog works better as a department effort than a solo project. If you've got something you're thinking about — an algorithm, a teaching problem, a technical argument, a half-formed idea — that's enough to start. The bar is: <em>would this be interesting to a smart person who isn't necessarily in your subfield?</em></p>

<p>Reach out to <a href="mailto:lundeenk@seattleu.edu">Kevin Lundeen</a>. I'll help you shape it.</p>
