<p>CS Perspectives launched in March 2026 with two pilot posts. Here's where we're headed — and what we'd love help with.</p>

<h2>Beautiful Algorithms</h2>

<p>A recurring series celebrating elegant solutions. Each post takes one algorithm, explains why it's beautiful, and connects it to something the reader cares about. Accessible to upper-division undergrads; interesting to practitioners.</p>

<p>Planned entries: <strong>Radix Sort</strong> (breaking the O(n log n) barrier by not comparing at all), <strong>Bellman-Ford &amp; Arbitrage Detection</strong> (negative-weight cycles = free money), <strong>Priority Queue</strong> (the most useful data structure nobody appreciates), <strong>Consistent Hashing</strong> (how the internet stays up when servers go down), <strong>Parallel Merge</strong> (the rank-based trick is gorgeous), <strong>Bitonic Sort</strong> (who thinks of this?), <strong>Embeddings</strong> (turning words into geometry), <strong>K-Means</strong> (so simple), <strong>Attention</strong> (of course), and <strong>Dissemination Barrier</strong> (minimal and complete).</p>

<h2>AI &amp; Teaching</h2>

<p>Posts about what AI means for how we teach computer science. The <a href="/2026/03/10/the-ai-knew-it-was-a-truck/">truck post</a> is the anchor; these extend the conversation into specific courses, specific assignments, and specific open questions.</p>

<p>Planned posts include <strong>Teaching Parallel Computing in the AI Age</strong>, <strong>Assigning Guided AI Activities</strong> (the practical "here's how I actually do it" piece), and <strong>Exploring Stochasticity</strong> (could we mimic evolutionary creativity in AI?).</p>

<h2>Conversations with Claude</h2>

<p>Posts structured as explorations — a question, a conversation with AI, and whatever emerges. The format itself is the point: demonstrating how to think <em>with</em> AI rather than defer to it.</p>

<p>Planned explorations: <strong>Could Bitcoin Be a Real-World Currency?</strong>, <strong>Could My Distributed Systems Students Shut Down Bitcoin?</strong> (a thought experiment that turns out to be a great way to teach distributed systems), and <strong>Resource Utilization Ethics: Bitcoin, LLMs, and the Cost of Computation</strong>.</p>

<h2>Collaborative Posts</h2>

<p>The blog should be a department effort, not a solo project. We're planning a <strong>Milsymbol Challenge</strong> with Stephen Riley (if he's game), and we'd welcome posts from any faculty member who wants to write about something they're thinking about, teaching, or building.</p>

<p>The bar is: <em>would this be interesting to a smart person who isn't necessarily in your subfield?</em></p>

<h2>Want to contribute?</h2>

<p>CS Perspectives is open to all faculty in the Department of Computer Science. If you've got an idea — or even just a half-formed thought you'd like to develop — reach out to <a href="mailto:lundeenk@seattleu.edu">Kevin Lundeen</a>. We'll help you shape it. The writing process is part of the point.</p>
